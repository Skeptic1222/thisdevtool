What is Aspect Ratio?

Aspect ratio describes the proportional relationship between the width and height of an image, screen, or frame. It is written as W:H — for example, 16:9 means the image is 16 units wide for every 9 units tall. Unlike absolute pixel dimensions, aspect ratio is scale-independent. A 1280×720 image and a 3840×2160 image both have the same 16:9 aspect ratio despite having very different pixel counts.

How GCD Simplification Works

The simplification algorithm uses the Euclidean Greatest Common Divisor (GCD). For 1920×1080: GCD(1920, 1080) = 120. Dividing both dimensions by 120 gives 16:9. The GCD is the largest integer that divides both numbers without remainder. This ensures the result is always the simplest integer ratio — no fractions or unnecessary large numbers.

Common Aspect Ratios and Their Uses

  • 16:9 (1.78:1) — Widescreen HD/UHD. YouTube, Netflix, broadcast TV, most modern monitors and laptops. Resolutions: 1280×720, 1920×1080, 2560×1440, 3840×2160.
  • 4:3 (1.33:1) — Traditional TV and monitor format. Still used in photography (medium format), video conferencing, and document scanning. Resolutions: 800×600, 1024×768, 1600×1200.
  • 3:2 (1.5:1) — 35mm film and most DSLR/mirrorless cameras. Standard for professional photography. Resolutions: 3000×2000, 6000×4000.
  • 1:1 — Square. Instagram posts, profile pictures, album art. Any resolution where width equals height.
  • 9:16 (0.56:1) — Vertical portrait video. TikTok, Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts, smartphone capture. Resolutions: 1080×1920, 720×1280.
  • 21:9 (2.33:1) — Ultrawide cinema and monitor format. Common in ultra-wide monitors and anamorphic cinema. Resolutions: 2560×1080, 3440×1440.
  • 2:1 — Dual-screen layouts, some cinema formats, panoramic photography.

Why Aspect Ratio Matters

Maintaining aspect ratio while resizing prevents image distortion — stretching or squishing that makes photos look wrong. When you crop or resize for a specific platform, knowing the target ratio lets you calculate exact pixel dimensions. Social media platforms have strict requirements: Twitter card images work best at 16:9, LinkedIn profile photos at 1:1, Pinterest boards at 2:3 (tall portrait). Responsive web design uses aspect-ratio CSS property to reserve space for images before they load, preventing layout shift. Understanding aspect ratio is also essential for print — a 4×6 print (3:2 ratio) requires different cropping from an 8×10 print (4:5 ratio).

Letterboxing and Pillarboxing

When you display content of one aspect ratio on a screen of a different ratio, black bars appear. Horizontal black bars at the top and bottom (letterboxing) appear when displaying widescreen content on a narrower display — common when watching 21:9 cinema on a 16:9 screen. Vertical black bars on the sides (pillarboxing) appear when displaying narrower content on a wider screen — common when watching old 4:3 TV on a 16:9 monitor. Understanding these ratios helps you design content that avoids unwanted letterboxing on your target display.
